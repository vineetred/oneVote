To see the difference in value better, look at how skewed it is in 2014.

The value of a vote in Andaman and Nicobar islands is 6.72 times that of a vote in Agra. While every vote is guaranteed to be the same, the value depends on how many electors there are in a particular constituency. One can also see that the value of a vote reduces over time too.

1952

The first ever Lok Sabha commenced with 489 seats.

1971

The last increase in Lok Sabha seats. Population has more than doubled since then.

1976

During the emergency, the Parliament passed a bill that essentially froze the number of seats in the Lok Sabha till the year 2000. This freeze was based on the 1971 census data.

2001

In the nth amendment, the Lok Sabha decided that there will be no increase in the number of seats till 2026. A freeze.

Malkajgiri and Lakshadweep

This is not even the starkest disparity.

The vote of a person living in Malkajgiri carries only 1/72 the value of a Lakshadweep resident's vote.


When the Parliament froze the number of seats in the Lok Sabha, the population in the Northern and the Southern states was more or less the same. However, 47 years later, this statement does not hold true anymore. In 1976, Parliament – then under the Emergency – passed a constitutional amendment that froze the number of seats each state had in Parliament as per the 1971 Census. The freeze was to end in 2000. But because it was so expedient, Parliament, in 2001, extended it till 2026. Thus, until 2026, Parliament’s seat composition will essentially represent a population snapshot taken more than 50 years ago.

The freeze has meant that the Hindi-speaking states such as Uttar Pradesh and Bihar, which have outstripped many other sates in population growth since the freeze, have not seen their seats in Parliament increase as they should have. The concept of one person-one vote does not hold true anymore.

 However, if we were to change and begin accomodating for the population increase since 71 (since which the population has almost doubled), we will start seeing the highly fertile Northern states of Bihar and UP get a lot more Lok Sabha seats than ever before. This might completely marginalise the Southern States, whose population or growth is much lower as opposed to their Northern counterparts, as the seats they have would be far lesser as opposed to now. The Southern states have a fertilty rate that is lower than that required for the replacement of their current population.
